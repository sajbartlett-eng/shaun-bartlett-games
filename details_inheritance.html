Fair division is mathematically hard.

Two siblings. One Estate. A battle of wits using the classic "Cut and Choose" protocol. This is a digital implementation of the fair division problem, forcing players to confront the difficulty of partitioning value in real-time.

System Design Analysis

How the Systems Literacy Framework is applied to game theory.

‚ö° Cognitive Economy (7c)

The Mechanic: Familiar Interface. The game uses the nursery rhyme logic of "I Cut, You Choose."

The Insight: We learn this rule as children sharing cake. Here, it is applied to a complex spatial grid. It creates a perfect Nash Equilibrium without explaining a single rule of math. The complex behavior emerges from a simple interface.

üß† Lean Decision Architecture (7a)

The Mechanic: The 60-Second Cut. Players have no inventory, no stats, and no dice.

The Insight: The game removes all administrative lag. The "Cognitive Budget" is spent entirely on spatial valuation and predicting the opponent's greed. High Agency, Zero Maintenance.

Rules Overview

The Three-Phase Loop

  • Phase 1: Develop (Blind) Players alternate placing assets (Manors, Barns, Sheds) onto the grid. Crucially, you do not know your role yet. You must build value without knowing if you will be the one dividing it or the one choosing it.
  • Phase 2: The Cut Roles are assigned via Rock, Paper, Scissors. The Cutter draws a single line across the estate. They want to make the piles equal to prevent the opponent from getting a better deal.
  • Phase 3: The Choice The Chooser picks the most valuable plot. The Cutter is left with the remainder.

Designer's Log

System notes and mechanical evolution.

üìì The Iteration: "Blind Development"

The Bug: When players knew their roles from the start, the Cutter would deliberately place obstacles to make the grid "uncuttable." This broke the game.

The Fix: I hid the roles until Phase 2. This forces players into a "Veil of Ignorance." You must build a valuable estate because you might be the Chooser, but you must keep it divisible because you might be the Cutter.

ü™û The Reflection
  • ‚Üí Greed vs. Fear: In Phase 1, did you place Manors (high value) or Sheds (low value)? Your choice reveals if you were playing to win (Greed) or playing not to lose (Fear).
  • ‚Üí The Cutter's Regret: If you lost as the Cutter, it wasn't bad luck. It was because you offered your opponent a better deal than you left for yourself.

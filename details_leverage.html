LEVERAGE

Power, Gold, and Betrayal

4-10 Players 15-30 Minutes Age 12+ Standard Deck

The old King is dead. Using only a standard deck of playing cards, form kingdoms, forge alliances, and betray your way to victory. Game of Thrones in 20 minutes.

Setup

Each player receives cards representing their power and gold. Kings are public, Nobles are hidden. Gold is secret. The game begins.

Three Victory Paths

King Victory

Be the last King standing. Eliminate all other Kings through warfare or assassination.

Wealth Victory

Accumulate the most gold when the game ends. Kings and Nobles compete for this.

Loyalty Victory

As a Noble, be allied to the winning King when they claim the throne.

The Core Tension

Kings need Nobles for military strength. Nobles need Kings for protection and legitimacy. But Nobles can betray at any moment‚Äîswitching allegiances to whichever King offers more gold or looks more likely to win.

Kings must balance:

  • Military aggression (eliminate rivals quickly)
  • Economic investment (accumulate wealth for endgame)
  • Noble loyalty (pay enough to keep them, but not too much)

Key Mechanics

Kingdoms & Allegiances

Nobles publicly declare which King they serve. This determines military strength. But allegiances can break‚ÄîNobles can switch sides, taking their power with them.

War & Assassination

Kings declare war by revealing their total military strength (personal cards + Noble cards). Loser is eliminated. Alternatively, Nobles can assassinate their own King‚Äîa risky betrayal that can swing the entire game.

Gold Economy

Gold changes hands through tributes, bribes, and war spoils. Hidden information creates paranoia‚Äîwho's actually richest? When should you strike?

What Emerges

The three victory paths create impossible tensions. A King focused on military dominance neglects wealth accumulation. A Noble hoarding gold has weak allegiances. Every choice sacrifices another path.

Players learn:

  • Power requires legitimacy‚Äîauthority without trust collapses
  • Multiple victory conditions make every alliance conditional
  • Betrayal isn't moral failure‚Äîit's rational strategy
  • Systems reward whoever best navigates competing incentives

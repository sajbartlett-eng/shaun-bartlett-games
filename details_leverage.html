LEVERAGE

A Card Game of Power, Gold, and Betrayal

The old King is dead. The Realm lies scattered.

Using only a standard deck of cards, Leverage models the fragility of feudal power. It is a game where the "strongest" player is often the most vulnerable, and where loyalty is simply a transaction waiting for a better offer.

üëë The Winning King Be the final ruler crowned. Requires spending gold to destroy rivals.
üí∞ The Wealthiest Individual Have the most Gold when the game ends. Requires hoarding gold and avoiding conflict.
‚öîÔ∏è The Loyal Noble Be a sworn protector of the Winning King. Requires betting on the right horse.

System Design Analysis

How the Systems Literacy Framework is applied to model political instability.

‚öñÔ∏è Structural Tension

The Mechanic: Gold is Ammo vs. Gold is Victory. To defend your crown, you must spend Gold in battles. To win the "Wealthiest" victory, you must hoard it.

The Insight: Power is expensive. The game creates a "Winner's Curse" where fighting for the throne often bankrupts you, handing victory to the Mercenary who stayed out of the fight.

The incentives for holding power are at war with the incentives for winning.

üîó Asymmetric Interdependence

The Mechanic: Crown Collapse. A King has a larger hand size (7) than a Noble (5), but if a King ever loses their last Noble, they lose the game immediately.

The Insight: The powerful are held hostage by the weak. A Noble can "Renounce" their King, stripping them of legitimacy. This gives the "weaker" player immense leverage over the ruler.

Power is not a trait of the leader; it is a resource granted by the followers.

‚öôÔ∏è Mechanical Honesty

The Mechanic: The Yield. In any battle, you can concede before cards are revealed to keep your Gold.

The Insight: Honor is a luxury. The system incentivizes rational cowardice. It is often mathematically optimal to surrender and stay rich than to fight and be right.

The game rewards survival instincts over heroism.

Rules Overview

Status & Treasury

Your political status dictates your Treasury (Hand Size Limit). If you exceed it, you must discard immediately.

  • Mercenary (3 Cards): The default state. Flexible but weak.
  • Noble (5 Cards): Sworn to a King. Protected, but subservient.
  • King (7 Cards): The ruler. High capacity, but fragile.

The Political Phase

Players use face cards to manipulate the board state:

  • Jacks (The Threat): Challenge a King. If you win, you steal their crown.
  • Red Queens (Cunning): Draw 4 cards. A rapid influx of resources.
  • Black Queens (Blackmail): Force a player to give you their highest Gold card.

The War of Kings

When the deck runs out, diplomacy ends. The remaining Kingdoms must battle until only one remains. Nobles must decide whether to fund their King's war with their own gold or let them fall.

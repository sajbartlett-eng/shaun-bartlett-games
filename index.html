The Systems Literacy Framework

A methodology for translating any complex human system into experiential learning.

Part 1: The Design Engines

‚öñÔ∏è 1. Structural Tension ‚ûï

The Principle: Incentives in Tension. The game creates incentive structures where individually rational choices lead to collectively irrational outcomes‚Äîmirroring real-world coordination failures.

Examples: Burnout (Promotion vs Team), The Tipping Point (Profit vs Crisis)
üîó 2. Asymmetric Interdependence ‚ûï

The Principle: Different Risks, Shared Fate. Players share the same goal but carry different capacities and costs. This disparity breeds resentment and necessitates negotiation.

Examples: Leverage (King vs Mercenaries), Winter Den (Bear strength vs Rabbit efficiency)
‚öôÔ∏è 3. Mechanical Honesty ‚ûï

The Principle: Systemic Indifference. The mechanics don't protect players from their choices. The same action can be cooperative or exploitative depending on context and intent.

Examples: Viral (Update mechanic forces sensationalism), Burnout (Delegate transfers stress)
üåä 4. Cascading Consequences ‚ûï

The Principle: Inevitability and Entropy. The system remembers your choices. A small error now permanently alters future probabilities, often creating a "race to the bottom."

Examples: Viral (Passing buries truth), The Tipping Point (Crisis Track)
‚è≥ 5. Scarcity Creates Pressure ‚ûï

The Principle: Systems thinking collapses under pressure. Constraints force players to abandon optimization and rely on imperfect heuristics.

Examples: Burnout (3 min timer), Leverage (Hand size limit)
üü• 6. Neutrality ‚ûï

The Principle: The System Must Reveal Reality, Not Preach It. A system should enable outcomes‚Äînot endorse them.

The Test: Can more than one moral path win? Does the system allow outcomes the designer dislikes? If yes, the system is honest.
‚ö° 7. Cognitive Economy ‚ûï

The Principle: Maximum strategic depth through minimum interface complexity. The "Cognitive Budget" is spent on calculation and negotiation, not rule upkeep.

a) Lean Decision Architecture Minimize administrative friction. One turn = one choice. No bookkeeping or phase management.
Example: The Tipping Point (Invest ‚Üí Roll)
b) Compressed Agency Players feel they can do anything, but only need to process one thing at a time. High perceived freedom, low load.
Example: Leverage (One card topples Kingdoms)
c) Familiar Interface Use tools (cards, pens, rhymes) people already know. Complexity emerges from player collision, not rule density.
Example: Inheritance (Nursery rhyme logic)

Part 2: The Pedagogical Outcomes

üí° 1. Emergent Insight

Players experience systemic dynamics firsthand before conceptualizing them abstractly. The realization ("I became a liar to win") emerges from experience rather than instruction.

üìñ 2. Narrative Culpability

Because the systems are open, players don't just watch the story; they are responsible for it. The story‚Äîand the guilt‚Äîbelongs to the table.

ü§ù 3. Strategic Empathy

By inhabiting the "bad guy" role, players learn that systemic failures are rarely caused by villains. They are caused by rational actors making logical choices within broken structures.

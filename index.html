The Systems Literacy Framework

A methodology for translating any complex human system into experiential learning.

Part 1: The Eight Design Engines

‚ö° 1. Accessible Complexity

The Principle: The Interface is the Trojan Horse. Use familiar, low-barrier tools to smuggle in deep systems modeling. Complexity comes from the collision of players, not the rulebook.

The Design: Using analog or intuitive interfaces to hide the math.

Examples: Inheritance (Nursery rhyme logic), Viral (Paper and pen)

üß† 2. Lean Decision Architecture

The Principle: High Agency, Zero Administrative Lag. The "Cognitive Budget" is spent on social calculation, not rule upkeep.

The Design: Immediate resolution loops (Choose ‚Üí Resolve) with no procedural overhead.

Examples: The Tipping Point (Invest ‚Üí Roll), Inheritance (Draw line ‚Üí Choose side)

üî• 3. Compressed Agency

The Principle: Maximum freedom, minimum mental tax. Players feel they can do anything, but only need to think about one simple decision at a time. Complexity belongs to the players, not the rules.

The Design: Tiny action sets with huge systemic ripples.

Examples: Leverage (Playing one Jack topples a Kingdom), The Tipping Point (One investment shifts global probabilities)

‚è≥ 4. Scarcity Creates Pressure

The Principle: Systems thinking collapses under pressure. Constraints force players to abandon optimization and rely on imperfect heuristics.

The Design: Hard limits on Time (Panic), Opportunity (Turns), or Capacity (Slots).

Examples: Burnout (3 min timer), Leverage (Hand size limit), Winter Den (7 rounds)

‚öñÔ∏è 5. Structural Tension

The Principle: The game explicitly places individual incentives at war with system stability. It weaponizes self-interest to reveal why "good people" create "bad systems."

The Design: Victory conditions that require selfish acts which degrade the shared environment.

Examples: Burnout (Delegate vs Team), The Tipping Point (Profit vs Crisis), Winter Den (Excess vs Collective Survival)

üîó 6. Asymmetric Interdependence

The Principle: Asymmetric Stakes, Symmetric Agency. Players share the same "verbs" but carry different risks, capacities, and costs. This disparity breeds resentment and necessitates negotiation.

The Design: Universal action lists where costs and impacts scale by role.

Examples: Leverage (King vs Mercenaries), Winter Den (Bear strength vs Rabbit efficiency)

üåä 7. Cascading Consequences

The Principle: Inevitability and Entropy. The system remembers your choices. Current actions permanently alter future probabilities, often creating a "race to the bottom."

The Design: Mechanics where an action now makes the same action easier (or harder) later.

Examples: Viral (Passing buries truth), The Tipping Point (Deregulation track raises future rewards)

‚öôÔ∏è 8. Mechanical Honesty

The Principle: Systemic Truthfulness. The simulation must be indifferent to the player's feelings. Every mechanic must truthfully model real incentives, not invented abstractions. No "fun" buffers.

The Design: Rules that punish altruism or reward exploitation if that is how the real-world system functions.

Examples: Viral (Update mechanic forces sensationalism), Burnout (Delegate transfers stress)

Part 2: The Pedagogical Outcomes

üí° 1. Emergent Insight

There are no lectures. The realization (e.g., "I became a liar to win") creates a moment of shock. It emerges naturally from the friction of the mechanics. The player discovers the lesson by committing the error.

üìñ 2. Narrative Culpability

Because the systems are open, players don't just watch the story; they are responsible for it. Leverage isn't a story about a Kingdom falling; it's a simulation where you let it fall to save your gold. The story‚Äîand the guilt‚Äîbelongs to the table.

ü§ù 3. Strategic Empathy

By inhabiting the "bad guy" role (e.g., the polluting CEO in The Tipping Point), players learn that systemic failures are rarely caused by villains. They are caused by rational actors making logical choices within broken incentive structures.

THE GREATER GOOD

The Predecessor to "Leverage"

Democracy dies when starvation begins.

Five plane crash survivors. One boat. Over 5 rounds, you must gather enough Food, Water, and Warmth to survive the journey home. But survival is not enough‚Äîyou also have secret Personal Agendas (recovering a wedding ring, saving a patient) that are worth points.

This is the Heavyweight Simulation that birthed the mechanics for Leverage. While Leverage abstracts power, The Greater Good simulates the scarcity that necessitates it.

The Core Tension: 34 vs 40

The group requires 34 Resource Cards to keep everyone alive. The deck contains only 40 Resource Cards[cite: 4, 5]. There is a surplus of only 6 cards. If any player hoards more than one extra card, someone else is mathematically guaranteed to die[cite: 6].

System Design Analysis

How the Systems Literacy Framework is applied to political economy.

‚öñÔ∏è Structural Tension

The Mechanic: The Death Penalty. If a player dies, the Leader loses -10 VP. Followers lose -3 VP[cite: 343, 344].

The Insight: Leaders need followers to survive, not out of kindness, but to avoid the massive point penalty. This models the "burden of command"‚Äîyou cannot win if your constituents die.

üîó Asymmetric Interdependence

The Mechanic: Role Scarcity. The Military Officer has Strength 5 (can dominate the endgame claim) but needs 7 resources to survive[cite: 367, 373]. The Nurse has Strength 1 but only needs 6 resources and draws huge amounts of supplies[cite: 391, 397, 409].

The Insight: The strong (Officer) physically need the efficient (Nurse) to feed them. The efficient need the strong to protect them.

‚è≥ Scarcity Creates Pressure

The Mechanic: The Hand Limit Hierarchy. Independents hold 3 cards. Leaders hold 7[cite: 34, 36].

The Insight: To hoard enough food to survive, you must join a coalition. Isolation is a death sentence. The system forces political engagement through biological necessity[cite: 361].

The Evolution of Leverage

The Greater Good introduced the "3/5/7 Hand Limit" mechanic. In this game, the limit represents Carrying Capacity (how much you can carry to the boat). In Leverage, it was adapted to represent Political Influence.

Power is a ladder built from selective accountability.

It's the end of the fiscal year. Your team must hit its sales target exactly. Miss it (run out of time), and the "weakest" performer is cut. Beat the target (overshoot), and the person who closed the deal faces the investigation alone. But if you hit it perfectly? That's suspiciously perfect‚Äîeveryone gets audited.

The Core Challenge

You are climbing the corporate ladder. You start with limited capacity (Hand Size 4). To win, you must be Promoted to Executive (Hand Size 6). But every promotion requires sacrificing your best card‚Äîleaving you vulnerable to the next round of cuts.

System Design Analysis

How the Systems Literacy Framework models bureaucratic blame.

‚öñÔ∏è Structural Tension (1)

The Mechanic: The Budget Cliff. Each Quarter has a specific target (10, 15, or 20 depending on player count). If you are under, the group continues. If you go over, the Quarter closes, and you (the player who played the last card) are now the Scapegoat.

The Insight: The system forces you to push your luck. You want to contribute to the quota to empty your hand (mandatory), but you never want to be the one who finishes it. Rational individual survival creates collective anxiety.

‚öôÔ∏è Mechanical Honesty (3)

The Mechanic: Suspicious Sales. Players can play cards "Face Down" to count as 10. If the card is actually Red, it is Cheating. If it is Black, it is just "bending the rules." The system accepts the input regardless of its truth.

The mechanic does not judge the lie. It only provides an Audit mechanism to reveal it later.

üåä Cascading Consequences (4)

The Mechanic: The Memory. Cheating cards (Red Face Down) stay on the table until the End of Year Audit. A shortcut taken in Quarter 1 remains a liability in Quarter 4.

The Insight: A small lie to close a budget early seems efficient now, but it permanently increases the "Audit Risk" for the entire group later. The system accumulates risk over time.

The Corporate Ladder

Your hand size represents your political capital. Losing hand size limits your agency.

4

Junior Staff

Starting State. You have few options and limited flexibility. You are frequently forced to play cards that hurt you because you have no alternatives.
5

Middle Management

The Transition. You have more capacity to hold Aces and high cards, but you are still one mistake away from being demoted back to the bottom.
6

Executive

Victory Condition. If you reach Hand Size 6, you win immediately. You have stopped managing the budget and started setting it.

The Pit

There is no elimination in Scapegoat, only humiliation. If you are Demoted below Hand Size 4, you drop to Hand Size 1. You have zero choices. You must play whatever card you draw. You are entirely at the mercy of the system until you can claw your way back.
